We consider $\mathbb{R}^n$ equipped with the dot product an example of an inner product space. Formally, an inner product space is a vector space $V$ over either $K = \mathbb{R}$ or $K = \mathbb{C}$ that is equipped with a dot product-like map $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle\colon V \times V \to K$ called an inner product. The inner product must satisfy the following properties:

  • Conjugate symmetry1: \(\langle \vec x, \vec y \rangle = \overline{\langle \vec y, \vec x \rangle}\) for all $\vec x, \vec y \in V$.
  • Linearity in the first argument: \(\langle a \vec x + b \vec y, \vec z \rangle = a \langle \vec x, \vec z \rangle + b \langle \vec y, \vec z \rangle\).
  • Positive-definiteness: \(\langle \vec x, \vec x \rangle > 0\) for all $\vec x \neq \vec 0$.

  1. [2] Prove that the dot product in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is an example of an inner product.
  2. [2] Prove that if \[A = \begin{pmatrix} e & f \\ f & g\end{pmatrix}\] is a symmetric real-valued matrix then the operation \(\odot_A\colon \mathbb{R}^2 \times \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}\) given by \[ \vec v \odot_A \vec u = \vec{v}^T A \vec{u} \] always satisfies conjugate symmetry and linearity in the first argument, but does not necessarily satisfy positive-definiteness.
  3. [1] Given an inner product space, we define the length of a vector \(\vec x\) to be \[ ||\vec x|| = \sqrt{\langle \vec x, \vec x \rangle}, \] and we define the angle between \(\vec x\) and \(\vec y\) to be \[ \angle\vec x\vec y = \arccos\left(\frac{\langle \vec x, \vec y \rangle}{||\vec x||\,||\vec y||}\right). \] Explain why this implies that we want our definition to assert that \(\langle \vec x, \vec x \rangle\) is a nonnegative real number and we why want \(\frac{\langle \vec x, \vec y \rangle}{||\vec x||\,||\vec y||} \in [-1,1]\).
  4. [1] Assume the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality, and use it to prove that \[ \frac{\langle \vec x, \vec y \rangle}{||\vec x||\,||\vec y||} \in [-1,1] \] for all \(\vec x, \vec y \in V\) when \(V\) is a vector space over \(\mathbb{R}\).
  5. [3] Now prove the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality either on your own, or by reading the Wikipedia proof by the orthogonal projection and reproducing it in your own words.
  6. [2] Describe the set of real-valued $2 \times 2$ symmetric matrices \(A\) such that \(\vec v \odot_A \vec v > 0\) for all nonzero vectors \(\vec v\). (These symmetric matrices are called positive-definite.) Prove that \[ \langle \vec v, \vec w\rangle = \vec v \odot_A \vec w \] is an inner product if and only if \(A\) is positive-definite.
  7. [3] Let \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) be a matrix with $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors. Prove that \(A\) is positive definite (i.e. \(\vec v^T A \vec v > 0\) for all nonzero vectors \(\vec v\)) if and only if all of the eigenvalues of \(A\) are strictly positive.
